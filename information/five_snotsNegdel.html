Horses are a big part of life in Mongolia. They’re not just animals but companions, and for centuries, Mongolians have depended on them for everything—from daily herding to long journeys across the steppe. The Mongolian horse might be small, but it’s incredibly tough, enduring the harsh winters and the scorching summers without much trouble. It’s this resilience that makes the Mongolian horse so special. Riding a Mongolian horse is also a cultural experience. There’s a deep connection between the rider and the horse, and Mongolians have mastered the art of horsemanship over centuries. Kids learn to ride from a very young age, and it’s common to see children as young as five racing horses during the Naadam festival. When you think about Genghis Khan and his empire, the Mongolian horse played a massive role. These horses carried his warriors across vast territories, often with little food and water. Their endurance and speed helped the Mongol cavalry become one of the most feared forces in history. In fact, many legends say that Genghis Khan’s horses could sense danger and even find their way back to their homeland after long journeys.

Beyond riding, horses also give much to the nomadic lifestyle. Mare’s milk, for instance, is fermented into airag, a traditional Mongolian drink enjoyed during the summer months. It’s refreshing, slightly sour, and a staple during festivals and gatherings. Herders also use horsehair to make musical instruments like the morin khuur (horsehead fiddle), an instrument deeply connected to Mongolian music and culture. In today’s Mongolia, while horses are still essential for herding, they’ve also become a symbol of pride and heritage. The annual Naadam festival celebrates the horse with long-distance races that test their endurance and strength, just as they did centuries ago.

Cattle play a huge role in Mongolian nomadic life, providing not only meat and milk but also leather, transportation, and even social status. Mongolian cattle are well-adapted to the harsh climate, grazing in the grasslands of the steppe, surviving the extreme cold of winter and the dry, hot summers. Unlike modern farmed cattle, they roam freely, finding their own food in the wild pastures. This freedom results in cattle that are leaner, tougher, and more self-reliant than their counterparts in other parts of the world. In the daily life of a herder, cattle are a reliable source of food. Milk, especially from cows and yaks, is essential for producing dairy products like tarag (yogurt), byaslag (cheese), and aarts (curdled yogurt). These products, stored and preserved, become a vital food source, especially in the long, cold winters when fresh food is scarce. Beef is also commonly used in Mongolian cuisine, from soups and stews to dried meat for long-term storage. Yaks, a close cousin to cattle, are often raised in the mountainous regions, particularly in the Altai and Khangai ranges.

Their thick fur makes them perfect for surviving colder climates, and their milk is richer and more fatty than cow’s milk, making it ideal for butter and cheese. Yak wool, soft and warm, is used to make clothes, blankets, and ropes. Historically, cattle were also used as a form of wealth and social status. A family with a large herd of cattle was seen as prosperous, and cattle were often traded or given as gifts to settle disputes or create alliances. Oxen, a larger breed of cattle, were once the primary source of transport for heavy loads, pulling carts across Mongolia’s vast plains. Though Mongolia’s cattle may seem simple in their role, they are a central pillar of the country’s nomadic culture and economy. Whether providing food, leather, or labor, cattle have sustained the Mongolian people for centuries, and they continue to be a vital part of life today.

Sheep are the most common and vital livestock in Mongolia, often referred to as the backbone of the herding economy. They provide multiple essential resources for the nomads, including meat, wool, and milk, making them indispensable to the Mongolian way of life. Their ability to thrive in the harsh climate, grazing on sparse vegetation, adds to their significance. Wool and Clothing: Sheep wool is used to create felt for traditional clothing, yurts, and various household items. It is especially valued for its insulating properties, providing warmth in Mongolia’s cold winters. The wool is processed into thread for knitting socks, sweaters, blankets, and the deel, the traditional Mongolian garment.
Meat: Sheep meat is a staple food, particularly during the colder months. It is used in various traditional dishes, such as boiled meat (chanasan makh), khorkhog (meat cooked with hot stones), and shul (soups). The tail fat, known as suul, is also highly valued and used in both cooking and as a stored energy source during winter. Milk and Dairy: Sheep's milk is rich in fat and is used to make various dairy products like yogurt, aaruul (dried curds), and butter.

Though not as prolific as cow's or yak's milk, it is still a valuable source of nutrition for nomadic families. Sheep also play a role in Mongolian herding traditions. During the winter, herders take special care to ensure the flock’s survival through the harsh weather, symbolizing the resilience and resourcefulness needed to thrive on the steppes. Sheep are typically herded alongside goats, which helps in maximizing the use of available pasture.

Goats are a vital part of Mongolia's five snouts, providing essential resources like meat, milk, and wool, especially cashmere, which is one of Mongolia’s most valuable exports. Mongolian goats are hardy animals, well-adapted to the country’s harsh climate, thriving in both the steppes and mountainous regions. Cashmere: Mongolian goats are world-renowned for their cashmere wool, which is prized for its softness and warmth. The fine undercoat of cashmere is collected by herders in the spring, when the goats shed their winter coats. Cashmere production plays a significant role in Mongolia’s economy, as the country is one of the largest producers of cashmere in the world. The fibers are spun into luxurious yarn and fabric, creating high-end clothing and textiles that are exported globally.

Meat and Milk: Goat meat is an important protein source in the traditional Mongolian diet. It is typically roasted, boiled, or used in stews, and it is a popular dish during colder months. Goat’s milk, though not as common as cow or mare’s milk, is rich in nutrients and is used to make dairy products like cheese and yogurt. Adaptability: Goats are extremely adaptable to Mongolia’s diverse terrain, often grazing on sparse vegetation in areas where other livestock struggle.

Their ability to forage in tough environments makes them a valuable asset to herders, especially in regions with less grass or harsher weather conditions. Goats also play a key role in leading herds of sheep, as they tend to seek out higher, cooler areas, guiding the flock to better pastures. Cultural Significance: Goats have a long history in Mongolian culture, and their wool, especially cashmere, has become a symbol of Mongolia’s connection to luxury and sustainable products. For nomadic herders, goats provide not just economic stability but are also deeply integrated into daily life, from food production to crafting.
